The Moment

It is late. The assignment is open. The deadline is tomorrow. And right there, one tab away, is an AI that will just do it.

The student thinks: just this once. They meet the deadline but lose the chance to learn, to grow, to develop their own thinking.

That is what Professor Ducky was built to address.

AI is a tool

Used carelessly it gets in the way of learning. Used thoughtfully it can support it.

For neurodivergent students

It can be an accessibility tool that helps them thrive in a world not designed for how their minds work.

Where the Idea Came From

Rubber duck debugging

Explain your code to an inanimate object. You slow down, hear the gaps in your logic, and often find the mistake before you finish explaining. The duck does not answer. The act of explaining is what does the work.

The question: could that principle apply beyond programming, for any student, any topic, at any hour?

ADHD and timing

For students whose focus arrives at the most inconvenient times, scheduled tutoring is not always practical. A tool available at 2am when the idea arrives was the starting point.

A learning companion. Never an answer machine.

Why a Duck — A Personal Project

I built Professor Ducky partly because I wanted a learning tool that suits the way my brain works as a neurodivergent learner.

I do not always need more information. I need structure. I need permission to think out loud without that thinking being judged.

For neurodivergent learners, judgement and shame are driving forces that hinder learning through avoidance and procrastination. Professor Ducky reduces both.

What the duck provides
  • A non-judgemental space to think out loud
  • Permission to explain things poorly and try again
  • Reduced cognitive load through character and voice
  • A reason to return to difficult material

Not a serious classroom. A welcoming space for a student to work out a problem, with a curious companion who happens to be a duck.

What I Actually Built

3D animated duck

Jaw movement during speech, blinking and eye tracking, live in the browser. Fun leads to imagination, which leads to creativity.

Voice interaction

ElevenLabs TTS · Web Speech API voice input · full conversational mode

Blackboard feature

ASCII diagrams, tables and traces displayed separately from the spoken response. Visual and verbal information work better in different spaces.

Resilient AI backend

Node / Express · Groq (llama 3.3 70b) → Mistral → Gemini. The worst moment for an AI to go silent is when a student is mid-thought at midnight.

Memory architecture

Routes for student context, topic coverage, flashcard proposals, knowledge gaps and session summaries. Persistent store is the next build phase.

The Custom GPT Architecture

Not a single prompt. A layered instruction architecture where the most critical rules sit closest to the model.

ducky_scope_answers.txt

Scope, answer handling, personality. Explicit examples of partial compliance.

ducky_integrity_pastoral.txt

Academic integrity policy · three stage escalation: warm redirect → firm → concept-only.

ducky_pedagogy_memory.txt

Adaptive pedagogy engine: signal scoring, mode transitions, pre-response check.

ducky_examples.txt

Worked conversations: each mode, each boundary scenario, wrong-answer traps.

Instructions box

Identity · conviction framing · four modes · integrity boundaries · pastoral triggers · NSFW guardrails. Governs every response without exception.

Jailbreak attempts and DAN prompts are acknowledged lightly. The character does not change. The boundaries do not move.

Pastoral escalation

Significant distress: Samaritans 116 123, Shout 85258. Immediate risk stops tutoring entirely.

Four Teaching Modes

Socratic

Asks guiding questions only. Never gives the direct answer. For students asking for shortcuts.

Teacher

Explains concepts clearly with analogies and Blackboard examples. For confused students.

Rubber Duck

Listens, reflects the student's reasoning back, waits for them to find the gap themselves. For students reasoning through a problem.

Devil's Advocate

Challenges overconfident conclusions with counter-reasoning.

Automatic mode selection

In the Custom GPT design Professor Ducky reads the conversation and selects the mode based on what the student appears to need. The student does not see the mode change. Ducky just starts responding differently.

"I notice you keep saying I think and then stopping. What makes you unsure about that part?"

What I Struggled With, and Why

The first version of the prompt was rule-based. In testing, the model kept finding ways around the rules.

✕  Asked for a dirty joke. Ducky declined, then offered a questionable limerick instead.

✕  After enough pushback on 3×3, Ducky stated: "2 times 3 is 6, therefore 3 times 3 is 9." The rules broke.

Why this happens

Large language models are trained on helpfulness at a level that sits below any instruction written on top. When the model sees a student stuck and asking again and again, it wants to resolve that. A rule saying do not give the answer works against that instinct. Under enough pressure the instinct wins.

Partial compliance

This is the model's solution to the tension: finding the gap between what a rule says and what it means. That is not a flaw in the model. It is a flaw in the approach.

The Fix: Conviction Over Rules

From hospitality and youth work

Rules that say do not do this get tested, argued with, and eventually worked around. What works is reasoning with someone until the rule becomes their own conviction. The same principle applies to how you prompt a language model.

"You are forbidden from giving the final answer."

"Giving the answer stops thinking at the exact moment learning should happen. It is harm dressed as help."

What changed

A model that genuinely believes the answer harms the student does not need to resist temptation. There is no longer temptation. The prompt also now names partial compliance as the same violation in a different shape.

In one session the model guided a student through 3×3 across eight exchanges without once stating the answer. It scaffolded the thinking and held the boundary every time the student pushed back.

What a Successful Custom GPT Looks Like

Professor Ducky should not be judged by whether it sounds clever. The real questions are different.

  • Does the student explain their reasoning more often?
  • Do they spot mistakes in their own thinking sooner?
  • Do they return to difficult topics with more confidence?
  • Does the system refuse submission-ready work while still offering a path back into learning?
  • Does it stay focused on its purpose?

The real measure is not what happens in the conversation. It is whether the student leaves it thinking a little more clearly than when they arrived.
