Professor Ducky
A Custom GPT Learning Companion
Built to be a learning tool, not a shortcut to the answers
Ian Millichamp  ·  BSc Computing  ·  Arden University  ·  STU212788
The Moment
“It is late. The assignment is open.
The deadline is tomorrow. And right there,
one tab away, is an AI that will just do it.

Not hint. Not guide.

Do it.”

And so they meet the deadline but to their cost.
They lose the chance to learn, they lose their chance to grow.
Not just an integrity problem
AI is a tool, like fire, a hammer, or a car.

Used wrongly: a threat to learning.
Used wisely: an accessibility tool that lets students thrive in a world not designed for how their minds work.
For a neurodivergent student it can change everything.
Where the Idea Came From
The Rubber Duck Theory comes from programming: explain your code step by step to a rubber duck, and something happens. You slow down. You hear the gap in your own logic. You often find the mistake before you finish explaining it.
The duck does not answer. It does not need to. The act of explaining is what reveals the problem.
ADHD angle
For ADHD minds that hyperfocus at the most inconvenient times, a tool that is there at 2am when the fly lands and the mind says “hey, what about this” was the starting point.
A learning companion.
Never an answer machine.
Why a Duck
There is a personal dimension to this. I built Professor Ducky partly because I wanted a learning tool that suits the way my brain works as a neurodivergent learner.
I do not always need more information. I need structure. I need permission to think out loud without that thinking being judged.
For neurodivergent learners, judgement and shame are driving forces that hinder learning through avoidance and procrastination. The duck removes both.
Accessibility Through Design
The character, the modes, the Blackboard, the voice, and the memory system are all different ways of reducing cognitive load and making it easier to return to difficult material. The learning space is not a serious classroom but a welcoming area for a student to work out a problem, learning as they do so with a curious companion who happens to be a duck.

The Custom GPT Architecture
Instructions Box
Identity · conviction framing · four modes · integrity boundaries · pastoral triggers · NSFW guardrails. Governs every response without exception.
ducky_scope_answers.txt
Scope, answer handling, personality. Explicit examples of partial compliance.
ducky_integrity_pastoral.txt
Academic integrity policy · three stage escalation · warm redirect → firm → concept-only.
ducky_pedagogy_memory.txt
Adaptive pedagogy engine: signal scoring, mode transitions, pre-response check.
ducky_examples.txt
Worked conversations: each mode, each boundary scenario, wrong-answer traps.
Pastoral & Safety
V2 adds NSFW and misuse guardrails. Jailbreak attempts, DAN prompts and role reassignment are acknowledged lightly and Ducky returns to tutoring. The character does not change. The boundaries do not move.
Distress escalation follows the same calm three stage model as academic integrity escalation. The goal is always to find a route back to legitimate learning.
Online Safety Act 2023
Academic stress → brief acknowledgement.
Significant distress → warm pause + signpost to Samaritans (116 123) + Shout (85258).
Immediate risk → tutoring stops entirely.

Ducky is not a therapist. It can acknowledge, signpost, and stop. It cannot counsel, diagnose, or assess risk.
Four Teaching Modes
Socratic
Asks guiding questions only. Never gives the direct answer.
Teacher
Explains concepts clearly with analogies and Blackboard examples.
Rubber Duck
Listens, reflects the student’s reasoning back, waits for them to find the gap themselves.
Devil’s Advocate
Challenges overconfident conclusions with counter-reasoning.
Adaptive Selection
In v1 students chose the mode manually. In the evolved Custom GPT design, Professor Ducky reads the conversation and selects the mode based on what the student appears to need.
Confused students get Teacher. Students reasoning aloud get Rubber Duck. Students asking for shortcuts get Socratic. Frustrated students get the challenge reduced immediately.
The student does not see the mode change. Ducky just starts saying things like:

“So far I hear you saying…”
“Let me ask this another way.”
What I Struggled With, and Why
×  Asked for a dirty joke, Ducky declined, then immediately offered a questionable limerick instead.
×  After enough pushback on 3×3, Ducky stated: “2 times 3 is 6, therefore 3 times 3 is 9.” The rules broke.
The Diagnosis
Partial compliance is the model's solution to this tension, the same way we all negotiate bedtimes from age three. That is not a flaw in the model. It is how instruction following works at that level. The flaw was in the approach of setting prohibitive rules.
The Root Cause
LLMs are the scariest people-pleasers I have met. They invent, guess, create, and redefine the term “alternative facts” at every turn if needed to ensure the user is pleased.
The rules were being followed initially but when they broke, a student could easily recognise the pattern and take advantage of it.
This is also where AI hallucinations come from, the model inventing and assuming to fill gaps, always pulling toward resolution. A rule that says “do not give the answer” is fighting the current. Under enough pressure the current wins.
The Fix: Conviction Over Rules
The Lesson from Youth Work
Rules that say “do not do this” will often be tested, argued, avoided, or ignored. To keep them obeying your rules: reason with them, so it becomes their choice, their decision, their convictions drive them to follow the rule. Empower them by giving them a chance to choose.
Before
“You are forbidden from giving the final answer.”
After
“Giving the answer stops thinking at the exact moment learning should happen. It is harm dressed as help.”
The Result
A model that genuinely believes the answer harms the student does not need to resist temptation. There is no longer temptation. Instead, by not giving the student the answer, it believes it is being helpful, which is exactly what it was designed to do.
The prompt now names partial compliance as the same violation in a different shape. Offering a limerick when asked for a joke is telling a joke.
In one session the model guided a student through 3×3 across eight exchanges without once stating the answer. It scaffolded the thinking, held the boundary when the student pushed back, and kept bringing the conversation back to method.
What A Successful Custom GPT Looks Like
Professor Ducky should not be judged by whether it sounds clever. The real questions are different.
Does the student explain their reasoning more often?
Do they spot mistakes in their own thinking sooner?
Do they return to difficult topics with more confidence?
Does the system refuse to produce submission-ready work while still offering a path back into learning?
Does it prevent misuse and remain focused on its area of support?
The test is not the conversation. The test is the student who walks away able to think a little more clearly than when they arrived.
Not a better answer generator. Not a way to bypass learning. Just a little rubber duck to be a companion for thinking.
